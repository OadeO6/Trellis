The Problem

The numbers that make
the case for change.

Nigeria's university system operates at a scale that breaks every traditional teaching workflow. These aren't edge cases — they're the norm.

📋

Assignment overload

Five minutes of feedback per student is 12.5 hours for a class of 150 — and over 100 hours for 1,200. That's two full working weeks just for one assignment.

💬

Communication breakdown

At OAU, 97% of 850 surveyed students used WhatsApp for academic info. Deadlines and announcements live in group chats institutions can't moderate or even access.

🎯

No individual support

LASU and University of Abuja report ratios of 1:114 and 1:122. At that scale, individualised support isn't difficult — it's structurally impossible.

🌙

No help at 11pm

Nigeria's private tutoring market is worth $250–400M a year — a market that exists precisely because the formal system doesn't answer questions when students need them.

Our Philosophy

Digital first.
Then AI.

Instead of asking "how do we apply AI to education," we start with a different question: how can the existing academic system be enhanced digitally?

Workflows that are currently manual, inconsistent, or scattered across WhatsApp groups and spreadsheets are brought onto a coherent digital system first. That layer is valuable on its own — it doesn't need AI to justify its existence.

AI is then introduced as an enabling layer — applied only to workflows that weren't possible before it. A tutor grounded in the teacher's own materials. An advisor that knows the student's actual transcript. An assistant that turns a photographed attendance sheet into a database entry.
